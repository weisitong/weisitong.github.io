Diodes

Semiconductor devices for rectification, protection, and voltage regulation

What is a Diode?

A diode is a semiconductor device that allows electric current to flow in only one direction. Think of it as an electrical one-way valve - current can flow from the anode (positive side) to the cathode (negative side), but not in the reverse direction. This unique property makes diodes essential for converting AC to DC, protecting circuits, and regulating voltage.

Diodes are fundamental building blocks in electronic circuits, found in power supplies, LED lighting, solar panels, and protection circuits. They come in various types, each optimized for specific applications like high-speed switching, voltage regulation, or surge protection.

Circuit Symbol

▷|

Triangle pointing to a line (anode to cathode)

How Diodes Work

Forward Bias

When anode is positive relative to cathode, current flows easily

  • • Low resistance path
  • • Voltage drop ~0.7V (Silicon)
  • • Current flows freely

Reverse Bias

When cathode is positive relative to anode, current is blocked

  • • High resistance path
  • • Only tiny leakage current
  • • Acts like open circuit

PN Junction

A diode consists of P-type (positive) and N-type (negative) semiconductor materials joined together. The junction between these materials creates a depletion zone that controls current flow direction.

Diode Types

Schottky Diodes

  • • Low forward voltage drop (~0.3V)
  • • Fast switching speed
  • • High efficiency in power applications
  • • Used in switching power supplies

TVS Diodes

  • • Transient Voltage Suppression
  • • Fast response to voltage spikes
  • • Protects sensitive components
  • • ESD and surge protection

Rectifier Diodes

  • • Convert AC to DC current
  • • High current handling capability
  • • Used in power supplies
  • • Available in various packages

Zener Diodes

  • • Voltage regulation in reverse bias
  • • Precise breakdown voltage
  • • Used as voltage references
  • • Overvoltage protection

Key Parameters

Forward Voltage (Vf)

Voltage drop when conducting in forward direction

Silicon: ~0.7V, Schottky: ~0.3V

Reverse Voltage (Vr)

Maximum reverse voltage before breakdown

Also called Peak Inverse Voltage (PIV)

Forward Current (If)

Maximum continuous forward current rating

Determines power handling capability

Reverse Current (Ir)

Small leakage current in reverse bias

Lower is better for most applications

Recovery Time

Time to switch from forward to reverse bias

Critical for high-frequency applications

Junction Capacitance

Parasitic capacitance at the PN junction

Affects high-frequency performance
