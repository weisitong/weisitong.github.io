Inductors

Magnetic energy storage components for filtering and power conversion

What is an Inductor?

An inductor is a passive electronic component that stores energy in a magnetic field when electric current flows through it. Think of it as the magnetic counterpart to a capacitor - while capacitors store energy in electric fields, inductors store energy in magnetic fields. It typically consists of a coil of wire wound around a core material.

Inductors resist changes in current flow, making them essential for filtering, energy storage in switching power supplies, and creating oscillating circuits. They're found in everything from simple chokes that filter noise to complex transformers in power supplies.

How Inductors Work

Current Flow

Electric current flows through the coiled wire

Magnetic Field

Current creates magnetic field around and through the coil

Energy Storage

Magnetic field stores energy, opposes current changes

Key Formulas

V = L × (dI/dt)

Voltage = Inductance × Rate of Current Change

E = ½LI²

Energy = ½ × Inductance × Current²

Inductor Types

Power Inductors

  • • High current handling capability
  • • Low DC resistance (DCR)
  • • Excellent thermal performance
  • • Used in DC-DC converters and power supplies

RF Inductors

  • • High frequency performance
  • • Low parasitic capacitance
  • • High Q factor for selectivity
  • • Used in RF circuits and filters

Common Mode Chokes

  • • Suppress common mode noise
  • • Dual coil construction
  • • High impedance to common mode signals
  • • EMI/EMC compliance applications

Ferrite Beads

  • • High frequency noise suppression
  • • Resistive at high frequencies
  • • Easy to implement in circuits
  • • Used for EMI filtering

Key Parameters

Inductance (H)

Ability to store magnetic energy, measured in Henries

Range: nH to mH

Current Rating (A)

Maximum current the inductor can handle safely

Based on saturation and thermal limits

DC Resistance (DCR)

Resistance of the wire used in the coil

Lower DCR means higher efficiency

Saturation Current

Current at which inductance drops significantly

Critical for power applications

Self-Resonant Frequency

Frequency where parasitic capacitance resonates

Limits useful frequency range

Q Factor

Quality factor indicating energy loss

Higher Q means lower loss
